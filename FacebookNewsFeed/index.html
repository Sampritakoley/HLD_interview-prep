01 / Understanding the problem

What is this system, really?

Imagine you have 500 friends and follow 50 pages. Every one of them posts things — photos, status updates, videos, links — at random times, all day, forever. News Feed is the system that collects everything your friends/pages post, decides which of those posts matter most to you right now, and shows you a personalized, infinitely-scrollable list the instant you open the app.

It is not a chat log and not a simple chronological list — Facebook stopped doing pure chronological feeds years ago. It's a personalized ranking + retrieval system that runs at the scale of billions of pieces of content and billions of users. Think of it like a personal newspaper reprinted uniquely for you on every refresh, except the "printing press" has milliseconds to do it, for 3 billion people.

Core features in scope

Post creation & storage

User creates a post — text, photo, video, or link — and it gets durably stored and made discoverable.

Feed generation

Given a user, produce the list of post IDs they should see, sourced from everyone they follow.

Feed ranking

Order and filter that list by relevance — an engagement prediction problem — not just recency.

Feed retrieval & pagination

Serve the ranked list back to the client fast, and let them keep scrolling without duplicates or gaps.

What makes this hard at scale

  • The fan-out problem. A regular user has ~200 friends. A celebrity like a sports star has 100M+ followers. Pushing one post into 100M feeds instantly is a write amplification nightmare.
  • Read:write ratio is extremely skewed. Feeds are read constantly — every scroll — but written to relatively rarely. You must optimize overwhelmingly for reads.
  • Personalization at scale. Ranking isn't a static sort; it's a per-user ML inference problem, computed potentially in real time.
  • Freshness vs. latency tradeoff. You want the newest posts, but computing "newest + best ranked from everyone you follow" on the fly for every scroll is expensive.
  • Infinite scroll without duplicates or gaps. New posts keep arriving while ranking shifts underneath the user, and offset-based pagination breaks under that.
02 / Requirements

Functional vs. non-functional requirements

Non-functional requirements are what actually drive architecture decisions — they're listed first for a reason.

Functional

  • Create posts (text, image, video, link)
  • View a personalized feed from followed people/pages
  • Feed ranked by relevance, not strictly chronological
  • Infinite scroll pagination
  • Like, comment, share — feeds back into ranking
  • Edit / delete posts, eventually reflected in feeds

Non-functional

  • Billions of users, billions of posts/day
  • Feed load < 200ms (p99)
  • Prioritize availability over strict consistency (AP)
  • Read:write ratio ≈ 100:1 or higher
  • Eventual consistency OK for feed propagation
  • Durability — posts must never be lost once acknowledged

Clarifying questions worth asking first

Is this a "friends" (bidirectional) model like Facebook, or a "follow" (unidirectional) model like Twitter? It affects fan-out design.
Do we need real-time push updates, or is pull-on-refresh acceptable?
Is ranking purely engagement-based ML, or do we also need a pure chronological fallback/toggle?
Does the feed include ads? Ad insertion changes the retrieval pipeline.
What's the maximum fan-out size — do celebrity accounts with 100M+ followers exist here?
Do we need to support media (photo/video), or just text posts for this design?
Multi-region / global users, or single data center scope?
Do deletes need to propagate immediately, or is eventual removal on next refresh acceptable?

Back-of-envelope math

Assuming 100M Daily Active Users. Order-of-magnitude accuracy is the goal, not precision.

Assumptions

DAU                      = 100M users
Feed opens/refreshes    = 10 per user per day
Posts created           = 10% of DAU post per day
Avg post metadata size  = ~500 bytes
Image posts             = 20% of posts, ~200KB each
Video posts              = 5% of posts, ~20MB each

QPS

Reads (feed loads):
  100M × 10                    = 1,000,000,000 (1B) reads/day
  Average QPS: 1B / 86,400     = ~11,600/sec
  Peak (3–5×)                  = ~35,000–58,000/sec

Writes (post creation):
  100M × 0.10                  = 10,000,000 (10M) posts/day
  Average QPS: 10M / 86,400    = ~116/sec
  Peak (3–5×)                  = ~350–580/sec

Read:Write ratio = 1B : 10M ≈ 100:1 → confirms heavily read-optimized system

Storage

Post metadata per day:
  10M posts × 500 bytes         = 5 GB/day
  Over 1 year: 5GB × 365       = ~1.8 TB/year

Media per day:
  Images: 10M × 20% × 200KB    = 400 GB/day
  Video:  10M × 5% × 20MB      = 10 TB/day
  Total media/day               = ~10.4 TB/day

Bandwidth

Feed load fetches ~20 posts, ~20KB payload per post (metadata + thumb refs)
Bandwidth per feed load      = 20 × 20KB = 400 KB
Total read bandwidth/day     = 1B × 400KB ≈ ~400 PB/day (metadata+thumbnails)

Conclusion: cannot serve from a single DB; need aggressive caching + CDN layers.

Component rationale

API Gateway / Load Balancer
Single entry point — handles auth, rate limiting, routes to the correct service, distributes load across replicas.
Post Service
Owns the lifecycle of a post — create, edit, delete. Writes to the Post DB, uploads media to Blob storage, then emits a "new post created" event to Kafka.
Post DB (sharded — Cassandra or MySQL)
Durable source of truth for post content and metadata. Sharded because 10M posts/day at scale exceeds what a single DB can handle for throughput or storage.
Social Graph Service + Graph DB
Answers "who does user X follow?" and "who follows user Y?" — needed both to fan a post out to the right people, and to pull from when building a feed.
Fan-out Service
Listens to "new post" events from Kafka and decides how to propagate the post into followers' feeds — push model — or leaves it to be pulled later.
Kafka (message queue)
Decouples post creation from fan-out/feed-update work. Without it, a celebrity post would try to synchronously update 100M feeds in the request path and time out instantly.
Feed Cache (Redis)
Stores precomputed, ready-to-serve feed lists — post IDs, sorted by rank/time — per user. This is what makes feed reads fast (milliseconds) instead of computing from scratch every time.
Ranking Service
An ML inference service that scores candidate posts per user based on features — relationship closeness, past engagement, post type, recency, predicted like/comment/share probability.
Blob/Object Storage + CDN
Media is huge and immutable-ish once uploaded, so it's stored separately from metadata and served from CDN edge nodes close to the user — never from the core DB/API path.

End-to-end data flow — "user creates a post"

  1. User hits "Post" in the app with a photo and caption.
  2. Client uploads the photo directly to Blob Storage, often via a pre-signed URL — this happens in parallel, not through the core API.
  3. Client calls Post Service with the caption plus a reference to the uploaded media.
  4. Post Service writes a row to the Post DB, sharded by post_id or author_id — this post_id, author_id, and timestamp is now durable.
  5. Post Service publishes an event PostCreated{post_id, author_id, timestamp} to Kafka.
  6. Fan-out Service consumes this event. If the author has fewer than ~10K followers, it does a push fan-out — looks up follower_ids from the Social Graph Service and inserts post_id into each follower's precomputed feed list in Redis.
  7. If the author is a celebrity with millions of followers, it skips push fan-out and marks the post available for pull-based retrieval later.

End-to-end data flow — "user opens their feed"

  1. Client calls Feed Service with GET /feed?cursor=...
  2. Feed Service checks Redis for that user's precomputed feed list, from push fan-out.
  3. Feed Service also separately queries for posts from any celebrities/high-fan-out accounts the user follows (pull path) and merges them in.
  4. The merged candidate list of post_ids is sent to the Ranking Service, which scores and reorders them.
  5. Feed Service fetches the actual post content for the top N ranked post_ids, and returns the assembled feed page plus a cursor for the next page.
  6. Media in the response is referenced via CDN URLs, fetched by the client directly from the CDN — not through the API servers.
05 / Database design

Storage layer

Multiple specialized stores, not one database — each part of the system has a different access pattern.

DataDatabaseWhy
Post content/metadataCassandra / sharded MySQLHigh write throughput, simple key-based lookups by post_id, horizontally scalable
Social graph (friends/follows)Sharded relational / Graph DBFast "who follows me / who do I follow" lookups, TAO-style optimization
Precomputed feed listsRedisFeed reads must be sub-10ms; in-memory is the only way to hit that QPS
Media (images/video)Blob/Object storage + CDNLarge binary blobs, immutable, best served via CDN, not a relational DB
Engagement countersDistributed counter storeExtremely high write volume on viral posts, needs eventual consistency

Posts table

CREATE TABLE posts (
  post_id      BIGINT   PRIMARY KEY,
  author_id    BIGINT   INDEXED,
  content_text TEXT,
  media_url    VARCHAR,   -- points to Blob/CDN
  created_at   TIMESTAMP,
  privacy      ENUM (public, friends, custom),
  status       ENUM (active, deleted, edited)
);

-- Sharded by author_id: co-locates a user's posts,
-- useful for pull fan-out bursts around a specific author.

Relationships / edges table

CREATE TABLE relationships (
  user_id       BIGINT,
  target_id     BIGINT,
  relation_type ENUM (friend, follows),
  created_at    TIMESTAMP,
  PRIMARY KEY (user_id, target_id)
);

-- Sharded by user_id so "who does X follow" is a single-shard lookup.
-- Reverse lookup ("who follows Y") needs a secondary/reverse-edge table.

Feed cache structure (Redis)

key:   feed:{user_id}
value: sorted set of (post_id, rank_score)
       — sorted by score, capped at ~1000 most recent/relevant entries

Engagement counters table (sharded by post_id):
  post_id, like_count, comment_count, share_count
  — approximate, eventually consistent

Sharding strategy

  • Posts — shard by author_id; co-locates a user's posts, useful because raw post reads often happen in bursts around a specific author's activity.
  • Relationships/graph — shard by user_id so the forward-follow lookup is a single-shard query; reverse lookups need a separate reverse-edge table.
  • Feed cache — sharded/partitioned by user_id hash across a Redis Cluster, so retrieval is a single-node operation.
  • Consistent hashing across all shards so adding/removing nodes doesn't require a massive reshuffle — only the neighboring partition is affected.
06 / Deep dives

Deep dive on 3 critical components

For each: the problem, the naive solution, why it fails, and the real solution used at scale. Volunteer these problems before the interviewer asks — it signals senior-level thinking.

01 Fan-out — push vs. pull (the celebrity problem)

The problem: When someone posts, that post needs to end up in the feed of everyone who follows them. How do you propagate one write into potentially millions of feeds without collapsing the system?

Naive solution — push fan-out for everyone

On every post, look up all followers and insert the post_id into each of their Redis feed lists synchronously.

Why it fails: For a regular user with 200 friends, this is cheap. But for a celebrity with 100M followers, one post triggers 100M Redis writes — enormous write amplification that overloads the cache tier, and if done synchronously in the request path, post creation itself would time out.

Real solution — hybrid fan-out
Normal users → PUSH (fan-out-on-write):
  New post → Kafka → Fan-out consumer
  → push post_id into each follower's Redis feed list, async
  → feed reads stay extremely fast, already precomputed

High-fan-out accounts (celebrities) → PULL (fan-out-on-read):
  Skip push entirely.
  At READ time, Feed Service separately queries
  "any new posts from celebrities I follow" from the Post DB
  / a celebrity-post cache, and merges into the feed.

Threshold (e.g. >10K followers = pull path) is configurable —
exactly the kind of number-based tradeoff interviewers want reasoned out loud.
This hybrid approach is what Facebook and Twitter actually use in some form — it converts an unbounded write-amplification problem into a bounded one.
02 Ranking — deciding what's "relevant"

The problem: Given 500–2000 candidate posts from everyone a user follows, decide which ~20 to show first, in what order, to maximize the chance the user finds it valuable.

Naive solution — sort by created_at DESC

Why it fails: A boring post from 2 minutes ago outranks a genuinely engaging post from a close friend from 40 minutes ago. Engagement and time spent drop compared to a relevance-ranked feed, and it doesn't scale personalization — every user sees the same order, just filtered by who they follow.

Real solution — multi-stage ML ranking pipeline
Stage 1: Candidate generation
  Cheaply narrow "everything ever posted by people I follow"
  down to a few hundred candidates using lightweight rules — fast.

Stage 2: Feature-based scoring
  Pull features per candidate: relationship closeness, post type,
  recency, predicted engagement probability, similar users' reactions.

Stage 3: ML model inference
  A trained model predicts engagement probability per candidate,
  served by a low-latency Ranking Service backed by a feature store.

Stage 4: Business logic re-ranking
  Diversity rules, ad insertion, integrity/safety filtering,
  freshness boosts.

Whole pipeline runs in tens of milliseconds by keeping feature
lookups in-memory and using a lightweight-enough real-time model.
03 Feed caching & infinite-scroll pagination

The problem: Users scroll indefinitely. Naive offset-based pagination assumes a stable, unchanging list — but new posts constantly arrive and ranking scores constantly shift underneath the user while they scroll.

Naive solution — offset-based pagination
SELECT * FROM feed ORDER BY score DESC LIMIT 20 OFFSET N;

Why it fails: Re-scans/re-sorts a shifting dataset on every page — expensive at scale, and if a new post is inserted above the current window mid-scroll, the user sees duplicate posts or skips posts entirely.

Real solution — cursor-based pagination over a snapshot
On feed open:
  Feed Service assembles and ranks a batch (e.g. top 200
  candidates) ONCE, returns an opaque cursor representing
  position in that ranked batch — not a numeric offset.

On each subsequent page:
  Cursor is passed back; server resumes from that exact
  position in the already-computed ranked list, stored
  temporarily in Redis, tied to that feed "session."

New posts arriving mid-scroll:
  Appended to future pages or surfaced via a "New posts"
  banner, rather than silently reshuffling the list.
This trades a small amount of freshness — you might not see the very newest post until you refresh — for consistency of the scroll experience and much lower recomputation cost. A deliberate, good tradeoff.
07 / Tradeoffs & alternatives

What we did NOT choose and why

Rejected alternatives

AlternativeWhy rejected
Pure push fan-out for everyoneCelebrity write-amplification problem — 100M+ follower posts would flood the cache tier and time out the request path.
Pure pull fan-out for everyoneKeeps writes cheap but makes every feed read expensive — querying every followed user's posts live, every time. Terrible read latency at a 100:1 read:write ratio.
Single relational DB for everythingPost write throughput, media storage volume, and read QPS each individually exceed what one DB instance can serve; different access patterns don't fit one storage engine.
Strong consistency (synchronous replication)Would slow down both writes (waiting for replicas) and reads (waiting for the latest data). Social feeds tolerate a few seconds of staleness fine — availability wins.
Pure chronological feed as the defaultUnder-performs on engagement/relevance; kept only as an optional user toggle, not the core design.

What breaks at 10× scale (1B DAU) and how we fix it

Problem — Redis feed cache runs out of memory / hot-key pressure
Fix

Shard further with consistent hashing across more nodes; introduce a tiered cache — hot users stay in memory, cold/inactive users' feeds computed on demand.

Problem — Fan-out write volume grows
Fix

Dynamically tune the push-vs-pull follower-count threshold based on real traffic; batch multiple follower-feed writes into fewer, larger operations.

Problem — Ranking service inference latency grows under load
Fix

More aggressive candidate pre-filtering before the model, approximate/cached scores for less-active users, and horizontal scaling of ranking replicas behind the feature store.

Problem — Social graph lookups slow down for supernode accounts
Fix

Cache follower/following lists for supernodes aggressively, since they change relatively rarely compared to how often they're read.

Problem — Kafka topic throughput bottlenecks fan-out events
Fix

Partition topics by author_id hash to parallelize consumption, and scale out fan-out consumer groups horizontally.

Problem — Cross-region latency as users become globally distributed
Fix

Regional read replicas/caches near users, with asynchronous cross-region replication of posts and feed data — again favoring availability over consistency.

08 / Summary

Cheat sheet

The ten things worth remembering before walking into the room.

What

Personalized, ranked, infinite-scroll stream of posts from friends/pages.

Core features

Post creation · Feed generation (fan-out) · Ranking · Retrieval/pagination.

Hardest part

Fan-out at scale — celebrity accounts with 100M+ followers.

Key NFRs

Read:write ≈ 100:1 · Favor Availability over Consistency (AP) · p99 < 200ms.

Fan-out strategy

Hybrid — push for normal users, pull for high-fan-out accounts (celebrities).

Ranking

Multi-stage — candidate gen → feature scoring → ML inference → business rules.

Pagination

Cursor-based over a snapshotted ranked batch, not offset-based.

Key databases

Cassandra/sharded MySQL (posts) · Graph store (social graph) · Redis (feed cache) · Blob+CDN (media).

Sharding keys

Posts by author_id · Graph by user_id · Feed cache by user_id, consistent hashing.

Biggest tradeoff

Freshness vs. cost — precompute/cache aggressively, accept seconds of staleness.

How to present this in an interview

Walk through the steps in this exact order, narrating your reasoning out loud at each decision point — "I'm choosing push fan-out here because..., but this breaks down for celebrities, so...". Interviewers are grading your tradeoff reasoning, not whether you memorized "the right answer." The numbers in Step 3 don't need to be exact — they need to justify the architecture choices from Step 4 onward. If you can explain why each component exists and what specifically breaks without it, you're demonstrating staff-level thinking, not junior-level memorization.
